A Brief Introduction to Crime

According to a Gallup poll from this year, over 50% of Americans consider crime in the United States to be a very or extremely serious problem.1 Boston lies in the top 50 of the 100 most populated U.S. cities in terms of violent crime rates.2 But can smart city planning and infrastructure reduce crime? What impact, if any, do streetlights have?

To answer this question, we leveraged Boston's open data platform and investigated nearly 265,000 crimes reported over the years 2016-2018. Scroll on to explore the trends, as we consider the effect of streetlights on mitigating violent crime in Boston.

Trends by Day and Night

After an orientation to the big picture trends, let's drill down into the data that's most relevant to the question of streetlights: time of day. Research in Houston by Rice University3 suggests that while crimes against property often coincide with well-lit areas like shopping malls and airports, number of crimes against people has an association with streetlight density. (Not necessarily a causal relationship, but correlated nonetheless.)

Does the phenomenon hold true in Boston?

The visualization below features 2016-2018 crime reports. We filter to assaults, injuries, and homicides, under the assumption that violent, public crimes against people are more likely to show seasonal and temporal patterns than crimes like fraud. Circle size depicts crime count and color accounts for changing sunrise and sunset times in Boston over the year.


While hourly trends show low levels of violent crime in the morning with a ramp through the day, they do not reveal an obvious association between crime counts and "streetlight hours" (dusk to dawn). However, the categorization of assaults, injuries, and homicides is subject to variability in officers' reporting and our own interpretation of the crime data descriptions. The data for shootings, on the other hand, is binary and clearer to interpret. When we filter explicitly to crimes where shootings occurred, we can see in the visualization below that counts are generally higher at night, specifically during summer months.

Beyond the Bulbs

What can this data teach us? Do streetlights really reduce crime? As our analysis shows, few violent crimes take place at night in well-lit areas, but on the whole, the majority of crimes occur during the day, when lights have no impact. While increasing the number of streetlights in poorly-lit areas of a city may prevent pockets of violent crime, it doesn't get to the root of the issue. If cities like Boston truly want to reduce their crime rates, they should focus on socioeconomic initiatives and crime prevention methods, like increasing the minimum wage and tightening gun control laws. There are other variables at play in geographical crime trends besides streetlight density, and as the Houston study suggests, the context of an area may be more important than the physical lights themselves.3
