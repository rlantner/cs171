Introduction

Everyone is concerned about crime in their area, a Gallup poll1 from this year showed that over 50% of Americans consider crime in the Unites States to be a very or extremely serious problem. But how does Boston stack up?

Boston lies in the top 50% of the 100 most populated cities in the United States in terms of violent crime rates.2 Let's delve into these trends and consider some potential methods to decrease the rate of violent crime in Boston.

Part 1: Crime report

Crimes are typically illuminated by news reports in real-time, presumably depending on the type of crime, the location and if and how the crime was reported. But what about the state of crime as a whole in Boston?

Part 2: Trends in crime by day and night

Research in Houston by Rice University3 suggests that while crimes against property often coincide with well-lit areas like shopping malls and airports, number of crimes against people has an association with streetlight density: more light, less crime. (This is not necessarily a causal relationship, but correlated nonetheless.)

Does the phenomenon hold true in Boston?

The visualization below shows the number of crimes committed over a three-year period from 2016 to 2018. We filter to assaults, injuries, and homicides, under the assumption that violent, public crimes against people are more likely to show seasonal and temporal patterns than crimes like fraud. Circle size depicts crime count and color indicates night, dawn, day, and dusk, accounting for changing sunrise and sunset times in Boston over the year.


While hourly trends show low levels of violent crime in the morning with a ramp through the day, they do not reveal an obvious association between crime counts and "streetlight hours" (dusk to dawn). However, the categorization of assaults, injuries, and homicides is subject to variability in officers' reporting and our own interpretation of the crime data descriptions. The data for shootings, on the other hand, is more cut-and-dry. When we filter explicitly to crimes where shootings occurred, we can see in the visualization below that counts are generally higher at night, specifically during summer months.








Part 3: Mapping streetlights and crime

As we can see there is an increase in violent crimes against people taking place during nighttime hours, what ways are there to mediate this? We can now look at crime locations vs. streetlight locations and evaluate whether streetlights have an impact on crime.

The below map of Boston shows all cases of homicide or instances of a shooting that took place during the nighttime hours between 2016 and 2018. You can select an individual instance to gain more insight such as the description of the crime, the date, and the location.

Now we need to visualize the proximity at which streetlights are to these instances of violent crime. The map to the left shows an isolated view of a crime-dense area of Boston near Nubian Square. If you click the button above, we can render the streetlights for this area.

Visually, we can already see that the majority of these instances occur in less proximity to streetlights, but more importantly the streetlight dense areas of this map have very few crime occurences. However, to further support this claim, we have calculated the average distance of crimes from streetlight locations in the following section.

Part 4: Shining a light on crime

Calculating the average distance between crimes and streetlight locations shows that, overall, crimes typically occur about 30 feet from a streetlight. Based on the previous research cited above, we predicted that crimes against persons would show greater average distances from streetlights than crimes against property. Our analysis reveals a more complicated pattern, as can be seen in the visualization below. The most violent crime type – those resulting in injury or death – does have the highest average distance from a streetlight (34ft). However, not all crimes against persons show this pattern. Assaults are in fact closer on average to the nearest streetlight (31ft) than are thefts (32ft). This suggests that streetlights may have a deterring effect on the most violent of crimes, but are not as effective at crime prevention as one might hope.

Conclusion

What can we learn from this data? It’s clear that streetlights do in fact have an impact on violent crimes that take place at night, but the fact is that most crimes in fact do take place during daytime hours, when streetlights have no impact. While increasing the number of streetlights in poorly lit areas of any city can prevent pockets of increased violent crime rates, every city should also be focussing on crime prevention methods such as increasing minimum wage and gun control initiatives.
